Most companies treat management as a necessary layer for coordination and development. This view is backwards.

Individual growth is driven by three things: ownership of real outcomes, direct contact with reality, and fast feedback from results. Add a manager between a capable person and those three elements, and growth slows immediately.

The manager becomes an information filter, a decision gate, and a political node. Information loses fidelity going up and coming down. Decisions take longer. Accountability diffuses. The person doing the work now optimizes for the manager's opinion instead of the actual problem. That single change kills learning velocity.

This is not a people problem. It is a system problem. Most managers are promoted because they were good at the prior job or because they are politically effective. Few are promoted because they increase the output and capability of everyone below them. The incentive structure rewards empire-building and risk avoidance, not the creation of autonomous high performers.

The result is visible everywhere. High-agency people either leave or get absorbed into the management layer where their personal output collapses. The remaining talent learns to navigate the hierarchy instead of mastering the physics, the code, the process, or the customer. Over time the entire organization compounds this mediocrity.

The physics is simple. Every additional layer increases latency and reduces signal. Every approval step adds friction. Every status meeting is time stolen from actual work. Organizations that keep adding managers are choosing slower learning and lower peak performance as a deliberate strategy.

The fix is not better management training or more sophisticated performance systems. The fix is fewer managers and a higher bar for the ones that remain.

Every manager should be able to do the core work of their reports at a higher level than the reports themselves. If they cannot, they are a net tax. Their job is context, ruthless prioritization, and removal of obstacles — not coordination theater or career coaching detached from output.

The best organizations stay as flat as physics and competence allow. Information moves at the speed of the people closest to the problem. Decisions happen at the lowest level that has the data and the accountability. People grow because they are forced to own hard things and live with the consequences.

Talent does not need managers to develop. Talent needs clear objectives, the right resources, and the absence of unnecessary constraints. Everything else is overhead that compounds over time.

If you want people to reach their maximum capability, stop inserting yourself between them and reality. Get out of the way or do the work yourself.
